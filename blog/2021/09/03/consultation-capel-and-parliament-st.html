Consultation: Capel & Parliament St.

Posted: Sep 3, 2021

A guide on how to give your response to the public consultation on continuing the pedestrianisation of Capel Street and Parliament Street. Closing date: 20th September 2021

If you’re not familiar with this trial, I’ll start with a quick recap. If you want to skip to how to fill out the consultation, click here.


Parliament Street as it was in 2017 - motor traffic continues to occupy most of the space on the street today. Image: hillofthirst, CC0, via Wikimedia Commons

The Trial So Far

In response to the pandemic, Dublin City Council asked the public for places where they would like more space given to active transport - walking, cycling, using mobility aids, scooting, and new transit like e-scooters. The single most requested change, with 10% of the overall submissions, was to make Capel Street car-free. However, the Council’s first proposal was underwhelming - motor traffic remained on the streets, with footpaths widened in only a limited number of places.

A public consultation on the plan received over 4,500 responses, and showed support from a majority of the public, residents, and business owners, with 85% of the public voicing support, and many requesting pedestrianisation. A new proposal was created, which included a car-free trial for 6 weeks in the summer - but only after 6:30pm on Friday, Saturday, and Sunday nights. Given the huge success of this trial, the car-free nights were extended for a further six weeks, after which a public consultation on the scheme was to be held in September.

However, when the announcement of the end of the trial towards the end of August resulted in many complaints, the trial was yet again extended to the end of September, continuing during the consultation. This is where we currenly stand.


So, What’s the Plan Now?

There are four general options in the consultation:

  • Return to what we had before, with no pedestrianisation.
  • Return to what we had before, including removing widened footpaths.
  • Make the trial arrangement permanent, with pedestrianisation after 6:30pm on weekend nights.
  • Make the streets pedestrianised 24/7 (with deliveries at set times).

Personally, I strongly support the third choice. This would allow a redesign of the streetscapes of Capel and Parliament Streets, without having to design around cars. We could plant trees and other greenery, which would help with pollution, sound dampening, and rainwater absorption. We could properly set out spaces for people passing though and those dining and drinking, allowing for easier social distancing and easier movement. The clutter of traffic lights and signs can be removed, high kerbs changed to a level and smooth street surface, and street furniture like public benches installed, all of which would make the streets more accessible to the disabled. Finally, with no cars, noise and air pollution will be reduced, and the street will be much safer for all, and dining outdoors will just be far more pleasant.

What about the effects on congestion - where will all the displaced motor traffic go? Surprisingly, it just stops existing! People will make trips at different times, or choose to take public or active transport instead, keeping traffic at the same balancing point as before. This phenomenon is often referred to as traffic evaporation, and is an extension of induced demand, a well documented effect. This is why building more roads doesn’t seem to help traffic: it encourages more and more people to drive until that tipping point is reached.

People often point to Irish weather, and speculate if people will be willing to dine outdoors year-round. However, continental cities like Copenhagen, Amsterdam, Brussels, and even Paris have plenty of pedestrianised streets and outdoor dining, and have roughly equivalent levels of rainfall in Winter. In fact, on average Paris actually has a greater number of rainy days than Ireland between October and June!

How to participate

The consultation page is here. Below a summary of the project there is a link to complete an “online survey” - click this to start the consultation.

It can be completed in under 2 minutes - to show support you need to click 7 buttons total. There is only one mandatory question. I’ve listed the questions I recommend you give your answer to below in bold. If you would like to support both streets being car-free 24/7, I’ve also made notes of which options support this. However, I’d recommend giving your opinion to as many questions as you’re able. I’ll give some inspiration below the list of questions, and you shouldn’t be shy about copy-pasting your own answers from one section into another if it applies to both.

Most importantly, you should be giving your own opinions here. This post is a guide on how to have your say, not on what to say. This is an opportunity for you to let the Council know what you think - just be honest!

Talking Points

Some issues that you might have thoughts on include:

  • Noise pollution from traffic
  • Air pollution, and the Climate crisis
  • Accessibility
    • Moving disabled parking to nearby and sensible locations
    • Providing public bathrooms and benches
    • Clearly marking cycle lanes with strong visual contrast and tactile paving/kerbs
    • Removal of street clutter like signs and traffic lights
  • Creating a safe environment for children and the elderly
  • Outdoor dining & drinking
    • Making the experience better
    • Providing more space
    • Safety
  • Bikes, scooters, etc.
    • Providing a safe corridor through the city
    • Maintaining access (not limiting street to only pedestrians)
    • Parking
  • The pandemic, and social distancing
  • Art and aesthetics
